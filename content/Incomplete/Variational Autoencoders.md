$$
\log p_\theta (x) = \log \int p_\theta (x|z)p_\theta(z) dz
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
    \log p_\theta (x) &= \log \int p_\theta (x|z)p_\theta(z) \dfrac{q_\phi(z|x)}{q_\phi(z|x)} dz \\
    &\ge \underbrace{\int q_\phi(z|x) \log \left( \dfrac{p_\theta(x|z) p_\theta(z)}{q_\phi(z|x)} \right) dz}_{Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO)} \\
    &= L(\theta, \phi ; x)
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
    L(\theta, \phi ; x) &= \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}\left[ \log p_\theta(x|z) \right]}_{ReconstructionTerm} - \underbrace{D_{KL}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p_\theta(z))}_{RegularizationTerm}
\end{aligned}
$$

1. The Reconstruction Term (The "Autoencoder" part):
    - Math: $\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}\left[ \log p_\theta(x|z) \right]$
    - Meaning: This encourages the decoder to assign high probability to the original input $x$ given the code $z$ sampled from the encoder.
    - In Practice: If $p(x|z)$ is Gaussian, maximizing log-likelihood is mathematically equivalent to minimizing Mean Squared Error. If Bernoulli, it is Binary Cross Entropy.
2. The Regularization Term (The "Variational" part):
    - Math: $D_{KL}(q_\phi(z|x), p_\theta(z))$
    - Meaning: This forces the encoder's output distribution $q_\phi(z|x)$ to look as close as possible to the prior $p(z)$ (the standard normal unit sphere).
    - Why is this crucial? Without this term, the encoder would "cheat" by making $q_\phi(z|x)$ a delta function (a single point) effectively memorizing the data (like a standard autoencoder). The KL term forces the encoder to inject noise and smooth out the latent space, ensuring that valid new data can be generated by sampling from the prior.
